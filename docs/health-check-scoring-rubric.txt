HOW TO USE THIS RUBRIC
-----------------------
After completing your review of the client's spreadsheet, score each
category using the criteria below. Add up the totals. Assign the
overall label based on the final score. Include the score card in
the PDF report with a one-sentence note per category.

Total Score → Label:
  80 – 100  🟢 Excellent
  55 – 79   🟡 Moderate Risk
  0  – 54   🔴 High Risk


════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
CATEGORY 1: FORMULA INTEGRITY                         (0 – 20)
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Are the formulas accurate, traceable, and free of critical errors?

  18 – 20   All formulas are clean. No errors. Logic is traceable.
            No hardcoded values where formulas should be.
            No circular references. No unexplained output.

  14 – 17   Minor issues only. Core logic is sound. A few hardcoded
            values or redundant formulas. No errors that affect output.

  8  – 13   Multiple hardcoded values. Some #REF!, #N/A, or #VALUE!
            errors — not all affect output but risk exists. Dependencies
            between tabs not clearly documented.

  4  – 7    Significant formula problems. Results cannot be fully
            trusted without manual verification. Multiple error cells.
            Circular references present.

  0  – 3    Critical failures. Formulas producing incorrect output.
            Business decisions based on this data are at risk.

What to look for:
  - Error cells (#REF!, #N/A, #DIV/0!, #VALUE!)
  - Formulas referencing external files that may have moved
  - Hardcoded numbers where calculations should be
  - VLOOKUP/INDEX-MATCH referencing ranges that could shift
  - Formulas that produce plausible-looking but incorrect results
  - Circular references


════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
CATEGORY 2: KNOWLEDGE TRANSFER                        (0 – 20)
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
If the person who built this left today, could anyone else run it?

  18 – 20   At least 2 people fully understand the system.
            It has been documented and successfully handed off before.
            A new user could get up to speed in under a week.

  14 – 17   2 people understand it at different levels.
            Minimal documentation but the second person could manage
            with guidance.

  8  – 13   Only 1 person fully understands it. A second person knows
            parts. No documentation. A gap would cause disruption
            but not total failure.

  4  – 7    Only 1 person understands it. That person built it and
            nobody else has ever used it independently.
            No documentation exists.

  0  – 3    Only 1 person understands it AND that person is
            retiring, transitioning, or has already left.
            Institutional knowledge is at immediate risk of loss.

What to look for:
  - "Only I know how this works" language from the client
  - Custom functions or macros with no explanation
  - Undocumented decision rules ("we just know to do it this way")
  - Tabs that nobody on the team opens or understands


════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
CATEGORY 3: SUCCESSION READINESS                      (0 – 15)
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Is there a plan for what happens to this system when ownership or
management changes?

  13 – 15   Written succession plan exists. Training materials or
            documentation are in place. A named successor has been
            identified and trained on the system.

  9  – 12   Succession is being actively planned. Some documentation
            exists. Successor identified but not fully trained.

  5  – 8    Owner is aware of the risk and has thought about it but
            no formal plan or documentation in place.

  2  – 4    No plan. No documentation. No named successor.
            Owner has not yet seriously considered the transition risk.

  0  – 1    No plan AND the triggering event (retirement, sale,
            health event) is imminent or has already begun.

What to look for:
  - Upcoming retirement, sale, or ownership change
  - Whether any training materials exist
  - Whether a successor has been named and trained
  - Whether the business could survive a 30-day absence of the owner


════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
CATEGORY 4: PROCESS DOCUMENTATION                     (0 – 15)
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Are the steps for using and maintaining this spreadsheet written down?

  13 – 15   All major processes are documented. Written SOPs or
            instructions exist for data entry, monthly updates,
            reporting, and version control. Documentation is current.

  9  – 12   Most processes are documented. A few gaps in less
            critical areas. Documentation is reasonably current.

  5  – 8    Some documentation exists but significant gaps. Critical
            steps (month-end close, data entry rules) are undocumented.

  2  – 4    Minimal documentation. A few notes or comments in cells
            but no structured instructions.

  0  – 1    No documentation of any kind. Everything is tribal
            knowledge held by one person.

What to look for:
  - Cell comments that explain processes (good sign)
  - A separate instruction document or README
  - Version history or change log
  - Manual steps that only happen in someone's head
  - Data entry validation rules (or lack of them)


════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
CATEGORY 5: DATA BACKUP                               (0 – 15)
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
What happens if the file is lost, corrupted, or accidentally overwritten?

  13 – 15   Automated backup runs on a schedule. File is stored in at
            least two locations (e.g. local + cloud). Backup has been
            tested and verified to restore. Version history is maintained.

  9  – 12   Regular backups exist but are manual or not tested.
            Cloud storage is used but versioning may be limited.

  5  – 8    Backups are inconsistent. The file is sometimes backed up
            but not reliably. No documented backup schedule.

  2  – 4    File exists in only one location. Manual saves only.
            Previous versions are not retained.

  0  – 1    No backup. Single file on one computer or drive.
            Loss of that device = loss of the file.

What to look for:
  - Where the file is stored (local drive vs. shared/cloud)
  - Whether OneDrive/SharePoint/Google Drive auto-versioning is on
  - When they last tested a restore
  - Whether "FINAL_v3_USE THIS ONE" naming convention suggests no versioning


════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
CATEGORY 6: REPORTING                                 (0 – 10)
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Are the reports this spreadsheet generates accurate, reproducible,
and auditable?

  9 – 10   Reports run automatically or with minimal steps. Output
           is consistent and auditable. Results can be traced back
           to source data. Someone else could reproduce the report.

  7 – 8    Reports work reliably but require a few manual steps.
           Mostly reproducible. Minor risk of human error in the process.

  4 – 6    Reports exist but require significant manual work or
           judgment to produce. Accuracy depends on who runs them.

  2 – 3    Reports are inconsistent or unreliable. Numbers sometimes
           don't match between versions or periods.

  0 – 1    No reliable reporting. Numbers cannot be independently
           verified. Reports are produced ad hoc with no consistent method.

What to look for:
  - How many manual steps are required to generate a report
  - Whether the output is consistent run-to-run
  - Whether there is a data source that can be audited
  - Manual copy-paste steps that could introduce error


════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
CATEGORY 7: SECURITY                                  (0 – 5)
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Who can access, edit, or delete this file and its data?

  5       File is password protected or access-controlled. Only
          authorized users can edit. Sensitive data is appropriately
          restricted. No unnecessary sharing.

  3 – 4   Limited sharing. Most users who have access should have it.
          Minor exposure (a few extra people have access who don't
          need it). No sensitive data fully exposed.

  1 – 2   File is shared broadly. Multiple people can edit without
          restriction. Shared folder with no access control.
          Sensitive data (client names, financials) visible to all.

  0       No security. File is on a shared drive, emailed as an
          attachment, or accessible to anyone with the link.
          No edit controls. History cannot be audited.

What to look for:
  - Is the file password protected?
  - Where is it stored and who can access that location?
  - Is it emailed around as an attachment?
  - Does it contain PII, financials, or confidential client data?

WRITING THE ONE-LINE CATEGORY NOTES
-------------------------------------
Keep each note to one sentence. Focus on the specific finding, not
the score. The score tells them HOW BAD. The note tells them WHY.

  Good:  "Two broken #REF! errors found in the Line 7 allocation tab."
  Bad:   "Formula integrity needs improvement."

  Good:  "Only one person knows how to run the month-end close."
  Bad:   "Knowledge transfer is limited."

  Good:  "File is saved to a local C: drive with no cloud backup."
  Bad:   "Backup practices are inadequate."

The note should be something the client could read aloud to their
business partner and immediately understand the risk.


WHAT TO INCLUDE IN THE REPORT
-------------------------------
The PDF report should include:

  1. Cover page with client name, date, file name reviewed
  2. The score card (total score, label, all 7 categories with bars)
  3. One-paragraph executive summary ("Here is what we found overall")
  4. Section for each category:
       - Score and label
       - What we looked at
       - What we found (specific — name the tabs, cells, issues)
       - Risk if unaddressed
       - Recommended next step (if any)
  5. Closing section: "What this means for your business"
  6. Appendix: build cost estimate (if remediation is warranted)

The score card visually should appear on page 1 or 2 so the client
sees their number before reading the details.
